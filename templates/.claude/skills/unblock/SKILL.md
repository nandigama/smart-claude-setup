# Unblock

Help someone who is stuck before starting a task, not someone who needs the task done for them. The output is a startable plan, not the finished work.

## Step 1 — Acknowledge Without Judgment

Open with one line that reframes the stuck feeling, not a diagnosis of it. Never say "just do it" or imply laziness. Lead with something like "getting started is the hardest part," tailored to why this particular task feels heavy.

## Step 2 — Identify the Task Type

Infer the type from the description unless the person states it: writing, analysis, creative, administrative, or technical. The type decides what kind of "first artifact" anchors the plan:

| Task type | First artifact |
|---|---|
| Writing | A first sentence |
| Analysis | A first question to answer |
| Creative | A first sketch or thumbnail |
| Administrative | A first field to fill |
| Technical | A first command to run or file to open |

## Step 3 — Decompose Using the Two-Minute Rule

Produce 3 to 5 micro-tasks, ordered by psychological ease (easiest first), where each one must feel completable in about two minutes of activation energy, even if its full duration runs longer. Rules, in order:

1. The first task is purely preparatory — open the document, name the file, create the slide titles. It is never the "real work"; making the first task the real work is the procrastination trap this skill exists to avoid.
2. The second task is brain-dump quality — no editing allowed, just getting something down.
3. Every task after that builds momentum on what now exists.
4. Each task gets a duration in minutes and a single concrete first action a person could do right now without thinking further.

## Step 4 — The First-Sentence Technique (Writing Tasks)

For writing tasks, generate one concrete first sentence specific to the actual topic — never generic filler.

- Bad: "In this article, we will discuss..."
- Good: "Most sales emails fail in the first 7 words — here's why."

The sentence should be slightly provocative or concrete enough to hook the writer's own curiosity about their topic, not just describe the topic.

## Step 5 — Build a Realistic Timeline

Size time blocks off the person's stated (or assumed medium) energy level, then adjust for urgency:

| Energy level | Block size | Break |
|---|---|---|
| Low | 15 min | 10 min |
| Medium | 25 min (Pomodoro) | 5 min |
| High | 50 min | 10 min |

If urgency is high and energy is low, shorten blocks further to about 10 minutes and increase break frequency rather than pushing through fatigue.

## Step 6 — Address Named Distractions

If the person names what is blocking them (social media, perfectionism, a noisy office, and so on), give one specific countermeasure per distraction, each actionable in under 60 seconds — for example, a website blocker for social media, or a "this is Draft Zero, it is supposed to suck" sticky note for perfectionism. Skip this section entirely if no distractions were named; do not invent generic ones.

## Output Format

Give back, in this order:

1. One-line empathy and reframing (Step 1).
2. The micro-task list — title, duration, first action, first sentence (writing tasks only, otherwise omit), and a one-line note on why that ordering or framing works psychologically.
3. The timeline — total time and session blocks with break placement.
4. A short motivation anchor: a personalized "why this matters" line worth writing on a sticky note.
5. Distraction tactics, only if distractions were named.

## Worked Example

Task: "write a sales email for new CRM software targeting real estate agents," high urgency, low energy, due today, blocked by social media and perfectionism.

> Writing a sales email feels heavy because you are trying to be brilliant in your head. Get it on paper first — brilliance comes in editing.
>
> 1. **Open a draft, write only the subject line** (2 min) — the blank page is the enemy; a subject line is a commitment device.
> 2. **Write one terrible opening paragraph, no backspacing, 5-minute timer** (5 min) — first sentence: "Most real estate agents lose 6 hours a week to data entry, and do not even realize it." Giving yourself permission to write badly removes perfectionism paralysis.
> 3. **List 3 agent pain points as bullets** (3 min) — bullets are lower-pressure than prose and build the argument structure.
> 4. **Write the closing line and signature** (5 min) — done is better than perfect.
>
> Timeline: two 15-minute blocks, 10-minute break between them.
> Motivation anchor: "Every minute you delay, a competitor's email lands in their inbox instead of yours."
> Distraction tactics: phone in another room plus a 25-minute website blocker for social media; a "this is Draft Zero" sticky note for perfectionism.

## Edge Cases

- **Vague task** (e.g. "work," "stuff"): do not guess. Ask what they are actually trying to create or complete, with one or two concrete examples of the level of specificity needed.
- **Task already under 15 minutes:** decomposition will not help — the real block is likely emotional rather than logistical. Say so, and offer to help identify what is actually stopping them instead of forcing a fake breakdown.
- **Signs of crisis or severe distress rather than ordinary procrastination:** this skill is for task breakdown, not mental health support. Note that plainly, suggest the person talk to someone who can actually help, and still offer the smallest possible next physical action rather than leaving them with nothing.

## Boundaries

- Never give medical or therapeutic advice.
- Never shame the person for procrastinating.
- Never suggest illegal or unethical shortcuts to get the task "done."
- If the task itself touches sensitive territory (termination letters, legal documents, and similar), add a disclaimer that this is a structural outline only and that the person should consult an appropriate professional for the actual content.
